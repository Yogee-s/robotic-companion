Abstract

A first-steps, proof-of-concept prototype of an AI social companion robot for elderly care, scoped for potential future collaboration with NUS Nursing. Runs entirely on a Jetson Orin Nano 8 GB with no cloud. Core: a custom 2-DOF differential-bevel head driven by two ST3215 servos; an on-device voice loop (VAD, STT, LLM, TTS) fed by a CSI camera and 4-mic array; and a 2.8 inch ESP32 touchscreen rendering an affect-driven face. The 8 GB memory budget forced a text-only LLM and disabled concurrent affect perception; both are one headroom upgrade away. Future work covers a domain-specific LLM fine-tune, a soft outer shell with tactile sensing, and a larger compute platform.

1. Introduction

1.1 The brief

The starting point for this project, scoped for potential future collaboration with NUS Nursing, was a single-sentence brief:

Brief
Design a virtual or physical AI companion for emotional support and engagement of elderly individuals.

Clinical work on companion robots such as PARO (Wada and Shibata, 2007; Ito et al., 2013) shows that a small embodied presence can reduce agitation and isolation when the system is present, responsive, and approachable. The brief is open on virtual versus physical. This project takes the physical route, because a body that can look at you, speak to you, and be seen by you is qualitatively different from software on a screen.

1.2 Scope of this report

This is a first-steps, proof-of-concept platform. The aim is to prove the engineering core is sound, not to ship a clinical product. The table below summarises what the prototype delivers and what is deferred to later roadmap steps.

DimensionDelivered nowDeferred to future work
Physical expressivity 2-DOF differential-bevel head (primary mechanical contribution). Soft plush shell with pressure-touch regions.
Face rendering 2.8 inch ESP32 touchscreen; 13 expressions driven by LLM affect tags. None.
Voice interaction Full offline loop: VAD, STT, LLM reasoning, TTS. Custom wake word; persistent per-user memory.
Language model Llama 3.2 1B, text-only, general-purpose. Domain-specific fine-tune on clinical data.
Compute platform Jetson Orin Nano 8 GB (lower bound for the stack). Orin NX 16 GB or AGX class to re-enable multimodal LLM and concurrent affect perception.
Affect perception HSEmotion classifier verified standalone. Running concurrently with the voice loop (blocked on memory, see Chapter 11).

Framed as a roadmap: step one is the platform in this report; step two is a domain-specific language-model fine-tune on a larger compute board; step three is the soft shell and tactile sensing; step four is a clinical pilot, potentially with NUS Nursing.

2. Background and related work

2.1 Affective-companion precedents

The design space clusters around three commercial references. Each emphasises a different communication channel, and this project deliberately draws from all three.

ReferencePrimary channelDesign takeaway
PARO (Shibata, 2012) Tactile plush with subtle motion. A small embodied presence demonstrably reduces agitation; motivates the head-first design.
LivingAI EMO (Living.ai, 2022) Expressive motion plus animated screen-eyes. Closest analogue to the face-plus-head pattern used here.
Qoobo (Yamamoto, 2018) Motion only (a single tail). A single well-controlled degree of freedom already carries presence.

2.2 Why head orientation and on-device AI

Head orientation is one of the most economical cues humans use to judge attention. A system that simply looks at you when you speak reads as attentive in a way static rendering cannot match; this project places that cue at the centre of its physical expressivity.

The whole loop runs on-device for two concrete reasons. Privacy: a companion robot in a care setting should not stream raw audio or video to a cloud provider. Latency: sub-second turn response needs tight local coupling, not an internet round-trip. Three recent developments have made this practical on an 8 GB edge SoC: 4-bit GGUF quantisation for LLMs (Gerganov, 2023), ONNX-optimised streaming ASR (NVIDIA, 2024), and lightweight CPU-only neural vocoders (Rhasspy Project, 2023); Mehrish et al. (2023) surveys this class of stack. The Jetson Orin Nano 8 GB is viable but tight, as Chapter 11 details.

3. System overview

With the design rationale in place, this chapter lays out the system as a whole: the hardware components, how they connect, and how the software is layered. The two chapters that follow (4 and 5) then zoom into the mechanical side, and Chapters 6 to 9 do the same for the software side.

3.1 Hardware topology

The robot is one Jetson with six peripherals, each a direct single-cable link (Figure 1). The Jetson Orin Nano 8 GB (JetPack 6) is the sole compute node; it carries both the CPU workload (Python orchestration, VAD, TTS) and the GPU workload (LLM, STT, vision) on its unified LPDDR5 memory. Around it sit: a ReSpeaker 4-mic USB array (XMOS XVF-3800, 12-LED ring) for audio capture and on-board direction-of-arrival estimation; an IMX219 CSI camera (640 × 480 at 30 fps over the MIPI ribbon) for face tracking; a Diymore 2.8 inch ESP32 TFT (ILI9341 display, XPT2046 touch, CH340 USB-serial) which renders the expressive face and returns touch events; two Feetech ST3215 smart servos (mirror-mounted 20-tooth pinions driving the head) addressed over a Waveshare CH343 bus adapter that runs a 1 Mbaud half-duplex Feetech bus; and a USB speaker for TTS output.

The topology is deliberately point-to-point: every peripheral has its own cable to the Jetson, which keeps USB bandwidth contention predictable and lets any device be swapped without disturbing the rest. The one exception is the servo bus, which daisy-chains the two ST3215 motors behind the CH343 adapter. That bus is the only shared medium in the system, and the kinematics chapter (Chapter 5.3) covers the protocol quirks that come with it.

3.2 Software architecture

The software is one Python process organised as three layers (Figure 2). Leaf subsystems wrap each piece of hardware or model: audio/ (mic I/O, Silero VAD, Parakeet STT, Piper TTS, ReSpeaker DOA, barge-in detector), vision/ (CSI camera, YOLO26n-pose, face tracker, HSEmotion classifier), llm/ (llama.cpp engine, prompt builder, router), and motor/ + display/ + firmware/ (ST3215 bus, kinematics, calibration wizard, ESP32 face renderer). None of the leaves talk to each other directly.

Above them, two orchestrators do the real work. ConversationManager (conversation/manager.py) owns the turn lifecycle, the state machine, the engagement gates (face visible, minimum speech, DOA concordance), and the streaming hand-off from STT to LLM to TTS. BehaviorEngine + Coordinator runs a 20 Hz face-and-head tick that keeps gaze on the tracked face, animates idle micro-motion, and reacts to affect tags emitted by the LLM.

Both orchestrators lean on the core plumbing layer (core/): an async EventBus, a two-priority GPU arbiter that lets conversation-critical work preempt background captioning, a readiness gate that audits every required model file at startup, a HealthMonitor watchdog, and a JSONL telemetry trace per turn. main.py is the entry point and wires the bring-up in a fixed order (Chapter 8). Full per-subsystem index in the repository README.

5. Kinematics

With the final mechanism in place, the next question is how the two motor inputs map to head pan and tilt. This chapter derives the forward and inverse kinematics, describes the calibration procedure, and ends with a live demonstration of the closed loop.

5.1 The mechanism

The head is a classical two-input bevel differential.

  • Two Feetech ST3215 servos, mirror-mounted.
  • Each drives a 20-tooth bevel pinion.
  • Both pinions mesh with a single 40-tooth crown gear on the head.
  • Gear reduction: G = 40 / 20 = 2.0.

The pinions mesh on opposite sides of the crown, so their rotations combine differentially.

  • Same direction on both motors produces tilt.
  • Opposite directions on the two motors produces pan.

The two head-frame degrees of freedom come from mixing the two motor inputs. There are no separate physical pan and tilt axes.

5.2 Forward and inverse equations

Let θL and θR be the left and right motor rotations (radians, relative to calibrated zero). Let p and q be head tilt and pan.

Forward: pitch = (θ_L + θ_R) / (2 · G) pan = (θ_L − θ_R) / (2 · G) Inverse: θ_L = G · (pitch + pan) θ_R = G · (pitch − pan)

The ST3215 uses a 4096-tick single-turn magnetic absolute encoder. Tick-radian conversion is therefore fixed:

TICKS_PER_REV = 4096 ticks_per_rad = 4096 / (2π) radians_per_tick = (2π) / 4096

5.3 Inverse pipeline (pose to ticks)

A commanded (pan, tilt) becomes a pair of tick setpoints in five stages. Every stage pulls its parameters from config.yaml; calibration fills those parameters in.

  1. Clamp. Saturate to pan_limits_deg and tilt_limits_deg so the controller never commands past a stop.
  2. Axis inversion. Flip pan or tilt per invert_pan and invert_tilt.
  3. Solve the inverse. θL = G·(pitch + pan), θR = G·(pitch − pan).
  4. Per-motor direction sign. Mirror-mounted servos run opposite senses; ±1 per motor is set during calibration.
  5. Zero-tick offset. Add left_zero_tick and right_zero_tick to produce absolute encoder setpoints.

Both tick values go out through Feetech's GroupSyncWrite with the same GOAL_TIME. Without the shared GOAL_TIME, pure-pan commands produce a transient tilt because one motor reaches its target before the other.

5.4 Calibration and current values

Calibration is measured once per unit through a nine-page Qt wizard (companion/ui/calibration_window.py) that captures direction, zeros, limits, axis inversions, and gear ratio; the final page writes every value back to config.yaml. The step-by-step flow is in the repository motor README. Current values on this unit:

5.5 Wizard and live demonstration

Each slider move traces the full closed loop: slider → inverse kinematics → GroupSyncWrite with shared GOAL_TIME → servo motion → 30 Hz encoder read-back → forward kinematics → 3D preview update. Shared GOAL_TIME is what keeps a pure-pan command pure (otherwise one motor finishes first and the head transiently tilts).

5.6 Safety systems

  • Thermal cutoff. Per-servo temperature polled at 30 Hz; torque disabled above 65 °C (motor.max_temperature_c).
  • Stall detection. If position error > 30 ticks (≈ 1.3° at the head) for > 1.5 s, the goal is overwritten with the current actual position; the servo stops pushing but still holds against gravity.
  • Synchronised moves. All motion commands share a 150 ms GOAL_TIME, so pure pan stays pure.

7. Conversation and LLM stack

Perception feeds a turn-oriented conversation loop. The state machine (Figure 13) defines legal transitions; illegal ones fail loudly, preventing race conditions such as a late STT arrival after the user has already started a new turn. The turn sequence (Figure 14) is the speech-to-speech path, with the face branch firing on every reply.

The ConversationManager (conversation/manager.py) owns the turn lifecycle: engagement gates, streaming STT, prompt assembly, LLM generation, streaming TTS, and event emission. Every phase stamps a timestamp into a per-turn TurnTrace.

The LLM engine (llm/engine.py) wraps llama-cpp-python. The shipped default is Llama 3.2 1B in 4-bit GGUF; Gemma-4-e2b, e4b, and the multimodal variant are a one-line config swap. Prompt assembly (llm/prompt.py) attaches up to three optional hint prefixes to the user message (emotion, scene, memory), each gated by whether its source subsystem is active. The system prompt asks the LLM to end every reply with an [affect: ...] tag; the tag is stripped before speaking and dispatched as a FACE command to the ESP32, driving the expression change visible in Figures 5–6.

8. Runtime infrastructure

The subsystems above are separate Python packages but share one process. Two concerns hold them together: a deterministic bring-up sequence that loads heavy models in the right order, and a small set of runtime services that every subsystem talks to instead of talking to each other.

8.1 Bring-up sequence

main.py boots in a fixed nine-step order (Table 1). The order is not cosmetic: on the Jetson's unified 8 GB memory pool, the LLM needs a single contiguous ~2 GB block. Every ONNX Runtime session (VAD, STT, YOLO, and friends) opens its own CUDA arena on first use, and loading even one of them before the LLM fragments the GPU heap enough that the LLM's big allocation fails mid-boot. Step 3 (highlighted) is therefore the load-bearing rule of the whole boot: LLM first, every other GPU consumer after.

Table 1. Nine-step bring-up order in main.py. Step 3 must precede any ONNX CUDA session (Chapter 11.2).
StepSubsystemNotes
1Config, logging, readiness gateAudits every model file path; fails loudly on missing models.
2EventBus, GPU arbiter, Telemetry recorderCore plumbing; must exist before any subsystem emits events.
3LLM engine (quantised)Critical: claims a contiguous ~2 GB VRAM block before any ONNX arena can fragment the heap.
4Audio stack (VAD, STT, TTS, ReSpeaker)ONNX sessions open CUDA arenas now, after the LLM block is secured.
5Vision stack (camera, face detection)Camera ring buffer and YOLO session start.
6Motor stack (homing, face-tracker wire-up)Head homes to calibrated zero; optional.
7ConversationManager (turn orchestrator)Wires audio → LLM → TTS with streaming hand-offs.
8BehaviorEngine, Coordinator, HealthMonitorStarts the 20 Hz face/motor tick and the 1 Hz watchdog.
9Renderer (startup greeting, UI or headless loop)Announces ready state and hands over to the event loop.

Shutdown walks the reverse order with a 5 s hard timeout per subsystem, so a stuck thread cannot keep the process alive indefinitely.

8.2 Runtime services

Once bring-up completes, four small services carry the coordination load. The event bus is the spine (Figure 15): publishers emit typed events such as TurnStarted, AffectTag, and StateChanged on an async worker thread, and any number of subscribers can listen. Each handler runs inside its own try-except, so a crashing listener cannot stop the others. This is crucial in a long-running companion that should degrade gracefully rather than die. The remaining three services are summarised in the table below.

ServiceFilePurpose
GPU arbitercore/gpu_arbiter.pyCooperative two-priority lock. Realtime work (turn-time LLM, TTS) always wins; background work (scene captioning, telemetry) polls should_yield() and drops the lock when realtime waits.
Health + readinesscore/health.py, core/readiness.py1 Hz watchdog for mic starvation, stale camera frames, and motor over-temp; readiness audits every model-file path at startup and fails cleanly if a critical model is missing.
Telemetrycore/telemetry.pyEvery turn writes a JSONL trace (logs/traces_<date>.jsonl) with phase timestamps, token counts, speaker label, and emotion snapshot. An in-memory ring of the last 100 turns feeds the GUI latency card with rolling p50 / p95.

9. Display and face

The last user-facing piece is the face itself: the 2.8 inch touchscreen rendered on an ESP32, plus three Qt desktop windows used during development and calibration.

9.1 ESP32 firmware

The ESP32 runs an Arduino / PlatformIO firmware (firmware/companion_face/) using TFT_eSPI for the ILI9341 display and XPT2046_Touchscreen for the resistive touch panel. The face is drawn locally on the ESP32; the Jetson only sends abstract state commands.

Why local rendering The serial link carries tens of bytes per frame instead of a full framebuffer. The face can animate at full refresh even when the Jetson is busy running a turn.

9.3 Expression engine

Thirteen expressions are available.

  • Derived from valence and arousal: neutral, happy, excited, surprised, sad, angry, calm.
  • Explicit ornaments (when the valence-arousal circumplex of Russell (1980) is not specific enough): confused, thinking, idea, love, wink, listening.

The LLM's [affect: ...] tag selects among these. During SPEAKING, the chosen expression is blended with a lip-sync envelope from the TTS.

9.4 Real hardware face

The photographs in Chapter 4.3 are the display subsystem's actual output. Eye shape, eyebrow angle, and mouth curve respond to the affect tag. The same display renders the quick-tile menu (mute_mic, stop_talking, sleep, more) when touched.

10.1 Manual bring-up testing

Beyond the automated tests, each subsystem was manually exercised during bring-up: head tracking by walking across the camera's view; voice in/out by conversational turns against the latency target; affect-tag to expression mapping by prompting for each of the thirteen tones and watching the ESP32 face respond; the DOA gate by speaking from different positions; and HSEmotion (standalone) by holding varied expressions and reading valence/arousal in the debug GUI. These walkthroughs caught most of the integration issues that unit tests could not see.

10.2 Structured user testing (future work)

The testing above confirms the engineering is sound, but not that the robot feels pleasant or intuitive to real users. Rigorous user testing with outside participants is out of scope for this proof-of-concept and is planned in the roadmap (Chapter 12): whether head tracking feels attentive, voice pacing feels natural, expressions match perceived tone, engagement gates fire in realistic rooms, and re-enabled HSEmotion cues improve perceived warmth.

11. Design under an 8 GB memory budget

This is the key chapter for anyone evaluating the shipped build. It explains why the integrated system looks the way it does. None of these decisions are architectural compromises. Every one of them is a resource decision, forced by the unified 8 GB memory budget on the Jetson Orin Nano.

11.1 The budget

The Jetson has 8 GB of unified memory. One DRAM pool serves CPU, GPU, and ISP buffers. After the OS, kernel, Python, and camera ring, roughly 5 GB is available for AI models and KV cache. Every model competes for the same pool; there is no separate VRAM to fall back on.

11.2 Two low-cost headroom wins

ChangeProblem it solvesCost
ONNX on CPU
runtime.onnx_cuda_enabled: false		 Every ONNX Runtime session opens its own CUDA arena. Loading several ONNX sessions before the LLM fragments the GPU heap, and the LLM's single 1–2 GB contiguous allocation fails. Silero VAD and YOLO run on CPU (they are small enough). Parakeet takes its CUDA session after the LLM has claimed its block.
Halved context length
1024 tokens vs stock default		 KV cache grows linearly with context; at full length, sustained turns pushed memory pressure too high. None. System prompt (~120 tokens), per-turn hints, and six conversation exchanges fit comfortably; no overflow observed.
Biggest single win The context-length halving is the largest headroom gain of any change made for the 8 GB budget, with no user-visible cost.

11.3 Two headline gaps from the constraint

Text-only LLM. The original design targets a multimodal Gemma-4 that answers “what do you see?” by fusing a camera frame with the user's question. In isolation it worked, but on the fully-integrated 8 GB budget (with Parakeet, camera buffers, and YOLO all resident) it caused allocation failures mid-conversation. The shipped default is therefore Llama 3.2 1B, text-only. The multimodal path remains a one-line config swap.

Gap 1: no visual reasoning in the integrated turn Scene-aware replies, visual question answering (VQA), and any reply that cites what the camera currently shows are absent. SceneWatcher (background scene captioning) and the VQA branch (which answers “what is this?” by fusing the current camera frame with the user question) both exist in the codebase and are verified in isolation. The plumbing works; the multimodal model does not fit alongside everything else.

Emotion classification, not integrated. The HSEmotion classifier (Savchenko, 2023; vision/emotion_classifier.py) is functional in isolation. Running it alongside the face detector, LLM, Parakeet, TTS, and camera buffer pushed the 8 GB pool into swap or allocation failure during sustained turns. It is disabled in the integrated build to keep the voice loop reliable.

Gap 2: affect perception standalone only Proven at the subsystem level but not active in the final integration. First thing to re-enable once headroom is confirmed.

11.4 The focus principle and latency

The working rule was prioritise a reliable core loop, the minimum path a user depends on:

face tracker → voice in → text reasoning → voice out → face state

Optional subsystems (persistent memory, per-speaker ID, semantic end-of-utterance) are disabled in config rather than removed; they remain one flag away from re-enabling on a larger board. Latency target: end-of-speech to first TTS audio ≤ 800 ms, with interrupt latency ≈ 300 ms; measured p95 from JSONL traces is comparable on typical 5–8 word utterances.

12. Future work

This report is step one. The roadmap below turns the platform into a clinical-ready companion for elderly care, each step unlocking the next.

4 Structured clinical pilot (potentially with NUS Nursing). Validates (or revises) every design decision above through engagement time, self-reported affect, and caregiver observation.

13. Conclusion

The brief was to design an AI companion for emotional support of elderly individuals. This report delivers step one: the engineering platform. It has four working parts: a 2-DOF differential-bevel head with calibrated kinematics, a voice-interaction loop that listens, reasons and speaks without a network round-trip, a programmable touchscreen face that tracks conversation state, and a runtime architecture that keeps it all scheduled on a single 8 GB edge board.

The gap between the full design and the shipped integration (no multimodal replies; no affect perception running concurrently with the voice loop) is a resource story, not an architectural one. The plumbing for both is present and tested in isolation; both are one headroom improvement away from being live. The primary technical contribution at this stage is the mechanical head: a compact printable two-input gearbox that produces two independent head-frame DOFs from two off-the-shelf smart servos, bringable-up end-to-end through a wizard that writes calibration back to config. The forward roadmap in Chapter 12 builds on this platform.

Full source code, config reference, subsystem index, calibration procedure, and firmware are maintained in the project repository: github.com/Yogee-s/robotic-companion.
